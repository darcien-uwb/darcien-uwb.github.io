Collaboration & Shared Leadership

Good leaders find opportunities for their colleagues to shine. They take responsibility for bad outcomes and share credit for good ones. They provide support, constructive feedback, and help resolve any team issues. Good collaborators listen to their colleagues. They share duties, assist each other, and hopefully, enjoy working together. Shared leadership is a combination of these: it inspires good teamwork by allowing each member to take ownership of a portion of the project, ultimately creating a quality, well-rounded product. The reflections below highlight my ability to collaborate and share leadership with my peers.

In the presentation “Making Wyoming Renewable: An Impossible Challenge”, my team discusses the feasibility of Wyoming becoming a renewable state by the year 2050. Countering drawdown solutions with the challenges of Wyoming being both politically conservative and coal and cow country, it was concluded that Wyoming most likely would not change its ways anytime soon. I participated in this group project during my first term at UW Bothell and did not know what to expect. As a returning student, I felt comfortable initiating discussion within the group. My personal leadership style is to allow others to select their preferred part of the project they want to own before I choose mine. I enjoy the challenge of filling in the gaps as it produces a new learning opportunity. We checked in with each other throughout the process, to assist and/or add encouragement when needed. While each member provided research and content for their section, I volunteered to take on the additional task of creating the final presentation, as I have a background in graphic design. Although new to Google Slides, I was successful in creating a cohesive and consistent look throughout the presentation, tying together all our hard work. We took turns presenting our sections to the class and were rewarded high marks.

“Spider-Man vs. Batman” is a group project where we were tasked to create a pop-up exhibit at a local museum. Brainstorming concepts led us to compare the worlds of Marvel and DC Comics, using the Museum of Flight as the venue. Requirements included presenting a concept document, usability report, storyboard, and media examples to industry experts, with the final step incorporating our information as a package. This was a big project and is a prime example of collaboration and shared leadership. Two members of the team possessed strong personalities and significant opinions while the remaining two members were more passive and agreeable. There were instances of managing control issues, where one member in particular attempted to do most of the project on their own, but we managed to work through them, ultimately dividing up duties, sharing responsibilities, and creating an amazing product. We elevated our project by being flexible, incorporating ideas from everyone, and allowing members to showcase their individual skills.

Writing & Communication

Good writing is inspiring. It creates a space to learn, disagree, or understand a different perspective. It tells a story, based on fact or fiction, and envelops the reader in that world. Most importantly, good writing has a message. Good communication is invaluable. It adds both credibility and clarity. It requires good listening skills and the efficient relaying of information. Good communication can be effective in many ways, be it by use of words, art, or a combination of both. In essence, good communication invites interaction and conversation. My first artifact is an example of transforming a data set into a story whereas my second artifact is an example of using art as a form of communication.

The paper “Data Dashboard: Project Proposal” describes the plan I use for my “Racial Demographics of Police and Population in the United States” data dashboard. Detailed sections include an introduction, project timeline, data biography, expected outcomes, mockup, and a reference page. I was drawn to this data set as it was relevant to our current world and I wanted to understand if the racial demographics of area police were proportional to that of the community they served. Stating statistics on their own can be dry, boring, and overwhelming. As a writer, it is important for the reader to understand why the data is interesting and draw them in to want to know more. This led me to tell a story of the LEMAS data set, researching its background and making connections to which people could relate. This project proposal far exceeded what I believed I could do, without leaving me feeling overwhelmed. It tells a story, is based on fact, and initiates discussion and communication among readers. It is a comprehensive example of my writing skills.

In “Mental Mapping of Your Home Community”, I create a geo-visual representation of the Columbia City neighborhood in Seattle, WA. Based on my personal perception of space, experience, and knowledge of Columbia City, I was tasked to be “true to yourself”. I initially found this assignment to be a bit intimidating, overwhelmed by the endless possibilities of how best to showcase my neighborhood. But then I got inspired. It was fun to reminisce about my old neighborhood, remembering my favorite eateries, best spots to view the lake, and varying pathways to historical buildings. I started creating my map by including these important data points, eventually filling an entire page of landmarks that were important to me. Although this assignment was for a mapping class, I got to draw and be creative, and not worry about scale or accuracy. Discovering alternative avenues to approach projects and employing non-traditional solutions generates interaction between the author and the reader. I look back at it now and am immensely proud of how I communicated my neighborhood to my peers.

Nelson, Darcie. “Data Dashboard: Project Proposal.” BIS 493 Advanced Data Visualization. Winter 2021.

In this paper, Nelson develops a proposal for a data dashboard project. Using a data set from Law Enforcement Management and Administrative Statistics (LEMAS), Nelson explores the claim “Do the demographics of area law enforcement agencies reflect proportionally to that of the population they serve?” She includes a description of the project, a project plan outlining task due dates, background information about the data set, expected outcomes for the project, and a mockup sketch of the data interface.

My intent for including this document as an artifact is to illustrate my skill in creating a comprehensive project proposal. This proposal demonstrates my style of writing and my abilities to both conduct research and create professional-looking mockups.



---. Data Dashboard: Racial Demographics of Police and Population in the United States. BIS 493 Advanced Data Visualization. Winter 2021.

In this project, Nelson creates a data dashboard to explore the claim “Do the demographics of area law enforcement agencies reflect proportionally to that of the population they serve?” Using R (a statistical programming language), Nelson develops an interactive web-based site allowing users to explore trends in the racial demographics of local police forces and the populations they serve. She includes bar charts comparing demographics from 2007 and 2016, and scatterplots to determine if the race of the police force is representative of the race of the population.

I chose to include this project as I am really proud of the finished project. I started the term feeling a bit overwhelmed and lacking in both my R language and GitHub skills, yet I was able to overcome my hesitation and create a dashboard that is interesting, provokes thinking, and informative.
